Developing Organizational Policies on Web Accessibility

Introduction

An organizational policy on web accessibility sets out your goals, practices, and processes for how your organization will support the creation of accessible websites and services. Your policy will make reference to recognized standards and guidelines, and possibly to national legislation.

By creating a policy organizations are taking a proactive step towards providing more inclusive services which is an important component of corporate social responsibility. Making your policy public, or even widely distributed internally, increases the visibility of activities and ensures accessibility is prioritized.

An organizational policy outlines what you aim to achieve, where as a conformance statement details what has actually been achieved. While your accessibility policy applies to your whole organization, you may have multiple conformance statements if your organization has many websites.

This document addresses considerations that can arise when developing organizational policies on web accessibility.

Reference guidelines and standards

Key actions

  • Identify and assess suitability of applicable standard and version.
  • Consider inclusion of authoring tool guidelines.
  • Specify what conformance level is to be achieved for each referenced guideline.
  • Assess the applicability of any standards to internal inclusion policies.
  • Consider any relevant legislative and regulatory requirements.

Examples

  • "This website aims to conform to W3C WAI WCAG Level AA."
  • "At ACME Inc. we seek to ensure all our websites and web applications, both customer facing and for internal use, conform all Level A criteria of the W3C WAI WCAG Version 2.0. We also aim to ensure that our authoring tools conform to all Level A criteria of the ATAG Version 2.0.

More detail

The W3C WAI produce recognized international standards specifying how to make websites accessible. The one most relevant is the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG), however, it is important to consider tools used in your organization and how they support creating more accessible content and users with disabilities.

Relevant guidelines

  • Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) - how to make websites accessible.
  • Authoring Tool Accessibility Guidelines (ATAG) - how to make software better support the production of accessible web content.
  • User Agent Accessibility Guidelines (UAAG) - how to make accessible browsers and multimedia players.

Authoring tools used to create websites can impact on the accessibility of the final product. You may consider guidelines for your organization's content creation tools as an important part of your policy statement.

W3C Guidelines categorize conformance criterion into levels; A, AA and AAA. Ensure your guideline reference includes an indication of the expected conformance level to be achieved.

You may also wish to reference relevant legislation covering inclusion and discrimination. For websites covering multiple legislative regions, it may be necessary to refer to the legislation for each region and possibly even differing standards. Note, you may also be subject to legislation as a result of your organizational domain, for example, education.

Related resources

  • Referencing and Linking to WAI Guidelines and Technical Documents - Provides guidance on references and links, along with WCAG links for policies, tools support, and others.

Define scope of policy

Key actions

  • Clearly specify what websites, pages, and components the policy applies to.
  • Specify how the policy relates to existing, updated, and new content.
  • Consider how the policy applies to third-party content and code providers.

Examples

  • "This policy applies to all new, updated, and existing web pages."
  • "This policy applies to all web content produced or updated by ACME Inc. In addition, ACME Inc. is taking the following steps to ensure accessibility of content provided by third-party developers:
    • Informing third-party developers of ACME Inc's policy on web accessibility;
    • Providing links to information and resources on implementing web accessibility;
    • Providing the incentives to providers of WCAG 2.0 Level AA conformant content.
    • Monitoring and providing feedback on inaccessible third-party content.
    • Seeking alternative third-party content providers where original providers continue to provide non-conformant content."

More detail

You organizational policy should clearly identify what website content it applies to. Consider to what extent the policy applies to new, updated, and existing web pages. Where your organization has multiple websites the policy should make it clear which websites are addressed. The version of your policy for site visitors need not refer to all websites covered but should outline what is covered on the website they are viewing.

The policy should specify to what extent it applies to web pages or components are supplied by a third-party. The website's users may need access to primary and to third-party content equally. It may take additional effort to educate and get compliance from third-party content developers.

Set milestones

Key actions

  • Specify when the guidelines and standards in the policy will be met.
  • Consider whether a staged approach might fix absolute barriers more quickly.
  • For large sites, consider prioritizing important features and functions.
  • Consider dates for support tools and internal resources.

Examples

  • "By February 27, 2015, ACME Inc's websites will meet WCAG 2.0, Level AA conformance."
  • "By November 7, 2014, ACME Inc's websites will meet WCAG 2.0, Level A conformance; and by May 29, 2015 websites will meet WCAG 2.0, Level AA conformance."
  • "By June 26, 2015, all vendors of authoring tools used by ACME Inc's should provide information regarding their plans for ATAG 2.0 conformance in future versions of their software. By June 24, 2016 ACME Inc. will preferentially purchase ATAG conformant authoring tools."

More detail

Where the websites in scope are known to fall short of the specified policy it is important to define a date by which they will meet the policy. This is an important part of accountability and ensures that focus is maintained on the policy targets.

When considering deadlines it is important to be realistic but also to ensure that issues are not left too long. In some cases it may be practical to phase in accessibility. First by rapidly addressing issues identified as absolute barriers. Then working solutions to other identified issues as part of the next round of site improvements. This approach would need to be clearly outlined within your policy.

For large websites it may be appropriate to priorities based on important features of areas of the site. For example, the checkout process of an online store. It may also be appropriate to identify particular types of content, such as video, and specify when they will be accessible. Consider also your authoring tools and processes and when accessibility barriers with them will be addressed.

Consider setting dates for establishing internal resources for training, technical assistance, monitoring, and/or an internal website with links to such resources.

Define monitoring, follow-up, and feedback process

Key actions

  • Regularly review milestones and update the policy.
  • Specify regular review period and process.
  • Define web page publishing workflow accessibility QA process.
  • Outline how visitor feedback will be captured, responded to, and used.

Examples

  • "All areas of the ACME Inc. website will be reviewed annually using the process described at Evaluating Web Sites for Accessibility. Reviews are the responsibility of those departments identified as 'owning' that area of the site. All new content that is published will be subject to accessibility checks as part of the publishing workflow."
  • "Each page of the website will include links for feedback on the site; this information will be compiled and considered during the review process. Any submitted feedback will be responded to within 48 hours."

More detail

Regardless of how the milestones are specified it is important to review progress to address known issues regularly. Any changes in timescale or, more ideally, milestones that have been met should be updated in the policy.

There is also a need to specify a recommended process and schedule for reviewing websites in scope to ensure that the target level of accessibility is maintained. This could be defined as regular reviews of websites or incorporated into the web page publishing process.

User feedback is important in understanding accessibility barriers. Your policy should include information on how this is captured and responded to.

Integrate and review policy

Key actions

  • Consider how your accessibility policy might be integrated into other organizational policies.
  • Regularly review referenced standards, guidelines, and legislation and update policies to always cite the most recent.

More detail

Your organization may have or may be developing an overall policy for websites. For example best practices for use of web standards, support for a privacy policy, internationalization, and usability. It is useful to incorporate accessibility into these overall policy, rather than to establish accessibility as a stand-alone policy. This ensures that accessibility is integrated into broader considerations and less likely to be considered as an afterthought.

Your policy is likely to reference particular standards or guidelines by version number, or make reference to particular legislation. It is important to ensure that you have mechanisms to review and update, or automatically update, your policies when the versions or legislation changes.

Example policies

Example of simple policy:

  • "[This organization] is committed to ensuring accessibility of its website for people with disabilities. All the pages on our website will conform to W3C WAI's Web Content Accessibility Guidelines 2.0, Level AA conformance."

Example of comprehensive policy:

  • "[This organization] is committed to ensuring accessibility of its website for people with disabilities. New and updated web content produced by our organization will conform to W3C WAI's Web Content Accessibility Guidelines 2.0, Level A conformance, by [date]. Existing web content produced by our organization, and new, updated, and existing web content provided for our site by third-party developers, will conform to Level AA conformance by [date]. We will initiate an internal monitoring program by [date]. Vendors supplying software used to develop our site will be required to provide information by [date] on conformance to W3C WAI's Authoring Tool Accessibility Guidelines [latest version], Conformance Level A. Our home page and our 'about this site' page will include links to this policy. We will review this policy in the future to consider updating it to reference later versions of W3C's accessibility guidelines."
